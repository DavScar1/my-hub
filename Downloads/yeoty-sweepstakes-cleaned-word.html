Introduction

A New Kind of Gambling

In October 2024, popular Twitch streamer Adin Ross live-streamed himself winning $1.5 million on Stake.us in front of hundreds of thousands of viewers. Videos like this have become common across YouTube, TikTok, and Instagram, where gambling content has transformed into mainstream entertainment.

High-profile sponsorships amplify this visibility. Stake.us sponsored F1 team Kick Sauber, while streamers like Adin Ross & xQc regularly broadcast casino gameplay to massive audiences. They even got the largest music artist in the world (Drake) to stream himself gambling. This visibility reflects a broader phenomenon: the rapid rise of sweepstakes casinos.

As of 2026, 48 U.S. states permit some form of gambling, with only Utah and Hawaii maintaining complete bans. Within this market, sweepstakes casinos have emerged as one of the fastest-growing segments, yet they operate in a legal grey area that raises important questions about consumer protection and market fairness.

What are Sweepstakes Casinos?
Sweepstakes casinos are online gambling platforms that exploit a legal loophole to operate without a gambling license. They use a dual-currency model. One currency labelled "Gold Coins" (for entertainment only), and another labelled "Sweepstakes Coins" which can be redeemed for real money. Since Sweepstakes Coins are given away for free when you purchase Gold Coins, platforms claim they fall under sweepstakes law rather than gambling regulation. This allows them to operate without a gambling license, avoid age verification, and skip consumer protections required of licensed casinos.

But does this legal distinction reflect reality? When users pay money, play luck-based games, and win cash prizes, the experience looks and feels like gambling regardless of how platforms label their currencies.

Aim

This project examines sweepstakes casinos through an economic lens. We will analyse how these platforms compete, how they market to users, what costs and benefits they create, and what regulatory approaches would best balance innovation with consumer protection.

We selected this topic after observing the proliferation of Stake.us branding across sports, entertainment, and social media. Our social media feeds were filled with massive gambling wins and we wanted to find out who was behind it and how all of this was legal. The goal is to understand whether this industry serves the public interest in its current form, or whether policy intervention could improve outcomes for users and society.

Pricing Analysis

With SC pricing converged at 1.00 per dollar and customer acquisition costs creating the primary competitive barrier, platforms compete aggressively on marketing to capture market share.

Key Finding: From the $5 tier onward, four of five platforms converge on 1.00 SC per dollar (±2%). Pricing is remarkably uniform. This suggests the market has reached competitive equilibrium where no platform can gain an advantage through price changes without triggering immediate competition.
SC Value per Dollar by Platform & Tier

Economic Interpretation. Why SC Pricing Converged

Nash Equilibrium¹⁸

The market reached a state where each platform's pricing is optimal given competitors' pricing. Any platform charging above 1.00 SC/$ loses customers. Below 1.00 SC/$ sacrifices margin without sufficient volume gain.

Implicit Price Collusion¹⁹

Through price leadership²⁰ and observation, all platforms converged on 1.00 SC/$ as an industry focal point that new entrants adopt automatically.

Cost-Based Constraints

If underlying costs (payment processing, regulatory compliance, redemption) are similar across platforms, equilibrium pricing naturally converges at the same rate.

Consumer Expectation Setting

The 1.00 SC/$ rate becomes a reference point. Customers expect this rate, making deviation above appear overpriced and below appear unsustainable.

Gold Coin Pricing Strategies (Differentiation Tool)

With SC pricing uniform, platforms use Gold Coin pricing as a differentiation mechanism and psychological marketing tool. Three strategies are visible:

Strategy 1: Aggressive GC Tiering :Chumba Casino

400% improvement from entry to standard tiers. Creates perceived value differentiation and encourages larger purchases. Essentially price discrimination²² on the entertainment product.

Strategy 2: Moderate GC Tiering :Pulsz

25% improvement from entry to top tier. Balances simplicity with some reward for larger purchases.

Strategy 3: Flat GC Pricing :Stake.us, McLuck, Global Poker

Rejects GC tiering entirely. Maximizes transparency and simplicity.

Psychological Functions of Gold Coins

  • Anchoring Effect²³: Large GC numbers make packages feel more valuable even when SC (the real value) is identical across platforms
  • Perceived Generosity: Platforms offering GC bulk discounts appear more generous, even though SC is priced identically
  • Loss Aversion²⁴: Players who use up GC balances experience psychological "loss," motivating additional purchases

The Paradox of Choice — Why It Doesn't Apply Universally

Behavioural economist Barry Schwartz documented how excessive options create costs. When consumers face too many alternatives: decision paralysis, decision fatigue, and post-decision regret all emerge.

Yet Stake commands dominant market share with 1,580+ slots. Three mechanisms resolve this paradox:

Information Architecture

The platform doesn't display 3,000 games simultaneously. Algorithms recommend games based on play history. Full catalogue remains accessible through search while casual users default to curated "featured" sections.

Customer Heterogeneity

Different users value variety differently. Casual players prefer Chumba's 220 curated games. Engaged users value Stake's 3,000 library. Users self-sort based on preferences. The platforms serve different market segments.

Satiation Effects

Frequent players exhaust content in smaller libraries within months. Once games become familiar, players switch. Stake's 1,580 slots solve this as even daily players encounter new games regularly, preventing boredom.

Barriers to Entry⁵

Despite $23.1B in market size and apparent profitability, market concentration remains high. 25+ new platforms launched in 2025 alone, yet the top five operators continue commanding the overwhelming majority of users. Three structural barriers explain this.

High Customer Acquisition Costs

Chumba's 500% first-purchase bonus means a $10 purchase delivers 40 SC total value (a $30 loss per customer before marketing costs.)

To achieve just 1% market share ($231M revenue) requires ~1.32M customers. At a 200% bonus rate: $26.4M in bonuses alone. With marketing spend: $39.6M total before reaching 1% share.

Game Licensing & Provider Relationships

Multi-year relationships with Pragmatic Play, NetEnt, Evolution Gaming create exclusive content unavailable to competitors. Volume-based revenue share means large platforms pay ~10% to providers vs. 15–18% for new entrants (a 5–8 percentage point margin disadvantage on identical games.)

On $50M annual wagers: new entrants pay $1.5M more than established platforms.

Brand Recognition & Time Barrier

Trust accumulates through demonstrated reliability over years. "Since 2012" (Chumba) signals financial strength. The same 500% bonus from an unknown new entrant signals desperation or potential fraud (identical action, opposite signal.)

Minimum 3–4 years needed to become a competitive firm in the industry.

Exception — Stake.us (founded 2022): Stake became dominant in under 4 years through a unique marketing strategy. Rather than competing on traditional acquisition, they created their own streaming platform (Kick), removed regulatory limits on gambling content, and deployed $200M+ annually targeting young male demographics. What Viewers See

Emotional Intensity

Streams feature unfiltered reactions. Screaming, jumping, intense excitement during wins. Emotional authenticity creates engagement scripted advertising cannot replicate.

Duration & Immersion

Rather than 30-second ad spots, streams run 3–4 hours. Hundreds of individual bets create an immersive experience where gambling feels normal and fun.

Selective Outcome Display

Big wins are highlighted and celebrated. Losses receive minimal attention or are framed as temporary setbacks. This creates availability bias⁵¹ (distorted perception of outcome probabilities.)

No Responsible Gambling Messaging

Traditional casino ads must include disclaimers. Influencer streams contain no warnings. Chat participants encourage continued gambling and celebrate wins without acknowledging expected losses.

The Parasocial Relationship³⁸ Mechanism

Viewers perceive influencers as friends or peers rather than advertisers. When Adin Ross gambles on stream, viewers don't process it as paid promotion (despite $24M annual compensation). They see someone they "know" participating in an activity, creating social proof³⁹ that gambling is normal and potentially profitable.

Research (Horton & Wohl, 1956) demonstrates viewers process influencer content as peer advice rather than advertising, creating trust that bypasses critical evaluation. The $200M+ influencer investment purchases trusted intermediaries who convert scepticism into participation through perceived authenticity.

Minor Exposure Scale

Age gates on streaming platforms rely on self-reported age with no verification.

  • FTC's 2002 survey found 100% of gambling websites allowed minors easy access

  • Spanish research: 6.5% of adolescents aged 12–17 engaged in online gambling (PubMed, 2019)
  • UK studies: ~50,000 children under 17 have used online gambling and developed problems (IDMERIT, 2024)
  • Finnish study: 13% of youth still gambled despite raising the legal age from 15 to 18 (PMC, 2020)

If just 5–10% of Kick's gambling viewers are minors bypassing age gates, this represents 5–10 million minor exposures annually from Adin Ross content alone.

Consumer surplus varies dramatically by user type. Recreational gamblers within budgets receive genuine value. Problem gamblers experience illusory surplus reflecting addiction rather than rational consumption. 32.4% are minors who cannot provide informed consent.

Chapter 4

Regulatory Analysis & Recommendations

Current regulatory status, the case for and against intervention, and our evidence-based policy recommendation.

4.1

Current Regulatory Status

Gambling requires three elements: payment, chance, and prize. Sweepstakes casinos eliminate "payment" through a simple trick: users buy Gold Coins (entertainment only) and receive Sweepstakes Coins as a "free bonus." Since Sweepstakes Coins are technically free, platforms claim no payment occurs for the gambling currency.

The legal fiction doesn't match reality. Only 12% of users spend money, and those who pay do so for Sweepstakes Coins, not Gold Coins. A $100 purchase gives exactly 100 Sweepstakes Coins (worth $100 in redemptions). The Gold Coins are the actual bonus, not the other way around. Platforms simply label things backwards to satisfy legal requirements.

Why Enforcement Fails

States rely on consumer protection laws, not gambling regulators. Attorneys general lack resources and expertise. Mandatory arbitration clauses block lawsuits. A California judge ruled in May 2025 that disputes must go to arbitration where platforms win almost all cases. Criminal prosecution faces jurisdictional problems when platforms operate overseas. No executive has been convicted as of February 2026.

4.2 – 4.4

Three Regulatory Options

Status Quo

No Regulation

The case for: Adults should choose their own entertainment. 80% of adult users know they're gambling. 88% play free. Market discipline works (bad platforms fail). Sweepstakes have been legal for decades (McDonald's Monopoly, Publishers Clearing House use similar models).

The case against: $2.4B extracted from minors annually. 32.4% of users are children. Market discipline has demonstrably failed to address these harms. Identical actions face different standards based on legal labels, not economic substance.

Net welfare: $6.5–10.9B positive. Minor protection: 0%.

✓ Recommended

Targeted Regulation

Six interventions correcting specific market failures:

  1. Real age verification (ID + database check)
  2. Mandatory loss disclosure (net losses displayed in real time)
  3. Self-imposed deposit limits (with 72-hr override cooldown)
  4. 24-hr cooling-off for deposits over $500
  5. Influencer marketing restrictions (mandatory disclosure, content rules)
  6. Shared self-exclusion registry across all platforms

Net welfare: +$11.4–16.8B. Minor protection: 99%+. Platforms operating: 150–180. Compliance cost: $360–600M.

Full Regulation

Full Gambling Regulation

Treat sweepstakes casinos as the licensed gambling operations they functionally are: state-by-state licensing ($5–10M per state), 15–30% gaming taxes, same age verification as licensed casinos, identical advertising restrictions, 1–2% problem gambling levy.

Concern: $5–10M licensing fees would consolidate market from 202 to ~20–30 platforms (Italy's experience: 93→46 platforms after stricter licensing). Further concentration in an already HHI 3,973 market.

Net welfare: +$11.3–16.9B. Comparable to targeted regulation but destroys 85% of operators. Annual compliance cost: $2.3–3.4B.

Policy Comparison — Key Metrics
4.5

Policy Recommendation

We recommend Targeted Regulation. This corrects specific market failures (information asymmetry, minor access, and behavioural manipulation) without eliminating consumer choice for informed adults. It applies established consumer protection standards to an industry currently evading them, achieving policy objectives through minimally restrictive means.

Why Not the Status Quo?

Survey evidence: 32.4% of users are minors. $2.4B extracted from individuals legally prohibited from gambling through verification requiring only self-attestation. 895,000 problem gamblers bear average annual costs of $11,966 while platform shareholders (including two billionaires worth $5.6B) capture $4.1B in profits.

Why Not Full Regulation?

Requires $5–10M state licensing fees, consolidating 202 platforms to ~20–30. HHI of 3,973 is already extreme. Further consolidation would eliminate competitive benefits driving innovation. Net welfare outcomes are virtually identical to targeted regulation ($11.4–16.8B vs. $11.3–16.9B), but at 4–6× the compliance burden. The modest additional welfare improvement doesn't justify destroying competitive market structure.

Implementation Strategy

Targeted regulation requires no new federal legislation. The FTC possesses existing authority under Section 5 of the FTC Act to address unfair and deceptive practices. Influencer disclosure requirements already apply to other industries. Age verification standards exist for online alcohol and tobacco sales. States can implement through consumer protection agencies.

Final Assessment

Conclusion

This project examined the sweepstakes casino industry. A $23.1 billion market exploiting a legal loophole to operate as gambling without traditional regulation. Through market analysis, survey research, and welfare economics, we asked: does this industry create a net positive or negative on society, and what should policymakers do about it?

What We Found

The market operates as a highly concentrated oligopoly¹². Stake.us commands 59.6% market share, with the top five platforms controlling 90% of users, yielding an HHI of 3,973. Despite this concentration, fierce non-price competition exists through welcome bonuses, VIP programs, and game selection (all with SC pricing stuck uniformly at $1.00.)

Our survey revealed 32.4% of users are minors bypassing age verification with a single click. Platforms invest over $200 million annually targeting young male audiences through influencer marketing, with 88.1% of users discovering sites through streaming and social media.

The welfare analysis shows $21.2–25.6 billion in annual benefits but $14.72 billion in costs. Benefits spread across millions of recreational users; costs concentrate on 895,000 problem gamblers losing $11,966 each, plus $2.4 billion extracted from minors.

The Core Problem

Sweepstakes casinos generate real value (entertainment, convenience, platform profits). But they generate this value by extracting wealth from people unable to protect themselves: children who cannot legally consent, young adults with underdeveloped impulse control, and problem gamblers trapped in compulsive cycles.

Traditional welfare economics says this is efficient because total benefits exceed total costs. But efficiency ignores distribution. Platform shareholders, including two individuals worth $5.6 billion, capture $4.1 billion in concentrated profits. Problem gamblers and minors bear concentrated harm. Three people suffer serious damage for every 100 who enjoy modest benefits.

What Should Be Done

Targeted regulation offers the best path forward: real age verification, mandatory loss disclosure, cooling-off periods, influencer marketing restrictions, and self-exclusion tools. This approach eliminates 99% of minor participation, reduces problem gambling costs by $5.6–6.7 billion, and improves net welfare by $4.9–5.9 billion over the status quo while maintaining 150–180 competing platforms at a compliance cost of just $360–600M annually (less than 10% of current platform profits.)

Final Assessment: Sweepstakes casinos create $6.5–10.9 billion in net positive welfare. But they create this value through a business model that systematically exploits those least able to protect themselves. Nothing can justify extracting $2.4 billion from minors or imposing $11,966 in average annual costs on 895,000 problem gamblers. Targeted regulation corrects market failures without eliminating consumer choice for informed adults. It's the policy most consistent with both economic efficiency and basic fairness.

Limitations

  • Survey sample (321 respondents) is small relative to 120M monthly users; online forum recruitment likely attracted younger users, possibly inflating the 32.4% minor figure
  • Cannot verify respondents' actual ages — under-18s might misreport; some figures could be higher or lower than reality
  • Accessibility benefit ($9.8B) assumes 25 casino trips/year
  • Opportunity cost calculations assume gambling money would otherwise be invested in the stock market (unrealistic for most people)
  • Problem gambling rates applied from general gambling research, not sweepstakes-specific data
  • Regulatory consolidation predictions based on Italy's experience. U.S. market is larger and structured differently
  • Connecticut and Delaware 2025 iGaming figures are estimated using single-month growth rates, which may not be representative
  • Platform revenue estimates derived from web traffic (SimilarWeb), not actual financial data. It assumes linear relationship between users and revenue
